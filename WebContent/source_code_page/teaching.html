Teaching Philosophy

I have strong interest in teaching activities and always feel good showing my peers what I knew, so I determine to choose teaching to be my life-long career. From August 2008 to May 2011, I worked as a teaching assistant for a wide range of courses, including Database, Geography Information System, Urban Geography, Word Cities, and American Geography. And later, in fall 2011, I became an independent instructor of INF 202 Database Concept and Design in my college. I not only survived in the first teaching semester, the positive results also encouraged me to teach diverse courses. Currently, as an adjunct faculty in my department, I am teaching three courses, two sections of INF 201 Web Technology and one section of INF 202 Database. I believe the teaching experiences and academic backgrounds accumulated in the college would enhance my future teaching career and also evolve as a college faculty.

First of all, I believe that teaching computer and information sciences in college is a necessity to prepare students inside and outside of the discipline for becoming competent generalists who may then develop the deep specialties demanded in this increasingly complex world. The majority of U.S. schools not only require that students can use computers but also expect students could use computer to explore millions of records using interactive tools to visualize and display data. The schools also prepare students to innovate and create the new technologies that drive local and national economies. Computer Science underlies most innovation today, from biotechnology to cinematography to national security. This ability to innovate with technology is also important for students' future success and ability to make a difference in a global society. Computer and information knowledge is one of the major elements of the necessary foundational knowledge of an educated person. Computer Science is also a very suitable field for teaching students to engage in complex thinking activities and develop reasoning skills from multiple perspectives, thus helping them advance not only their specialized disciplinary abilities but also proving helpful in their everyday lives as well.

In addition, by teaching technical courses, I would like to guide students to think of the problems they meet as challenges in real world such as an IT product or a website, because students can understand a problem thoroughly if they connect it with something they are familiar with. In summary, education in computer sciences goes far beyond telling students where things are-rather; it is to help create generalists with specific specialty; it is to provide a powerful perspective for logical analysis and valuable skills; and it is also to enlarge the scope of thinking by adding considerations into everyday life.

In terms of teaching practice, I believe a good course should go beyond lecturing and discussion and that the intellectual level should be higher than just understanding and remembering. I understand the role of good instructional design in successful teaching. When I plan to teach a course, I will make sure I have clear objectives for students to achieve, know how to assess the extent to which those objectives are achieved, and choose relevant teaching activities consistent with both the objectives and assessment.

Moreover, I am also a believer in the use of hands on exercises and diverse teaching methods in teaching. I like to get a variety of teaching methods, such as slide lectures, group exercises, team based learning (TBL), online courses, in class exam, and take home exams. Different teaching styles stimulate the interests of students and facilitate good learning. I also lay emphasis on hands exercises in teaching because students will learn real knowledge when they are really doing something. However, the exercises should be organized and presented in a way not to become overwhelming.

I also like to bring my own research into the classroom when I think it is relevant and useful. This connection usually attracts positive attention from students and it is a good activity to teach students how to conduct academic research. For example, my dissertation is General Transit Feed Specialization data visualization and application, in which I need to build the database in MySQL and present data on web by HTML, CSS and JavaScript. Therefore, I presented my research in both classes. In database class, I describe the process how to build the GTFS database and how to retrieval data from database in a web project; on the other hand, in Web Technology class, I present how to use div tag build the web structure. It proved a very successful exchange with the students.

Finally, I believe fairness and caring about students are also necessary for success in teaching. I am glad when I hear students say I am doing well on these aspects of the relationship that develops between student and teacher.

Overall, it is always a great feeling when I can help students grow intellectually and mentally through my efforts; and such a sense of achievement is very rewarding to me. Although it usually takes me so much time to prepare for lectures, exercises, and other assignments, and have to interact with students at the same time, interest, responsibility, and enthusiasm always encourage me to keep a good balance in the different aspects of life. One of my strengths is that I am always looking for something new to learn and keep improving myself during the process, and I am confident this will sustain me through my teaching career.

3-Year Teaching Experience and Interests

Instructor: INF 201 Web Technology & INF 202 Database

Course Description:

Currently, I am teaching two sections of INF 201 Web Technology and one section of INF 202 Database at State University of New York, Albany. Both are 3 credits undergraduate core courses in the Department of Informatics. All three courses are arranged in the same computer lab with 24 seats. All three courses are full enrolled by either computer science undergraduate or information sciences undergraduate. Before this semester, I taught INF 201 in Spring 2013 and INF 202 in Fall 2011, Spring 2012 and Fall 2012. In retrospect it was a great experience. I still remember, in 2011, how excited I was when some students told me they would not know it was my first time teaching if I had not told them. I always examine and generalize my teaching in the middle of semester and by the end of semester. Also, I always update my teaching contents in the following semester. For example, in INF 201 I add Google Maps API introduction this semester which was not covered last Spring; in INF 202, I start to use MySQL as DBMS this semester. In the previous semester, Access was the major tool, which actually is not a complete database management system. I am also trying different teaching methods. In the past five semester, my teaching methods include slides presentation, individual exercises, group exercises, team base learning and online courses. These teaching experiences gave me a good foothold from which I can continue to improve; but at the same time, I also felt a sense of achievement as an instructor and I am determined to make college teaching as a major focus of my academic career. Informal Student Responses:

After the course I sent e-mails to a random group of students to ask for their feedback about their learning and my teaching during the whole semester in order to obtain valuable information to benefit my future teaching. Most students found the materials in the course interesting and expressed they learned more when exercises were given during the class. I always insert some exercise during the class to help students digest what they just learned and regain their attention. They also liked my generalization talk before class that usually helped them to review the knowledge in last class and realize the agenda of current class. Last semester, in 201 Web Technology, Some students felt my demo was a little fast and sometimes I tried to feed too much information in a limited time. Part of reason is that last spring was first time teaching for Web Technology. This is what I am working on - how to manage time and the pace of a new course. I am positive it will be better as my experience accumulates. In summary, looking for direct feedback from students has been a major element in my instructional development. While I am glad to hear encouraging words about how my students learn well from the course, I also welcome critics from whom I can learn.

Working as Teaching Assistant for Multiple Courses

From August 2008 to May 2011, I worked as a teaching assistant in Department of Informatics and Department of Geography and Planning at State University of New York, Albany. I helped in a wide range of undergraduate courses including Database Concept and Design, Geography Information System, World Cities, Urban Geography, American Cities, China Geography. Thanks to the diversity of subjects, I have learned from a variety of experienced professors rich subject knowledge, useful teaching skills, and how to interact with students effectively.

My work usually included preparing course materials, grading homework and exams, answering student questions during office hours, leading database labs, and helping students with projects. Occasionally I gave lectures, usually because the instructor missed the class for some reasons. I give a lecture regarding China Urban Planning and Development every fall semester in World Cities. This TA experience has laid a solid foundation for me to become a college teacher. During my first time teaching as an independent instructor, whenever I met some problems, I looked back to my TA experience for potential solutions and most of the time I could find a good one. In retrospect, I really enjoyed my TA experience and have been grateful to those warm-hearted professors who helped me greatly in personal development.
